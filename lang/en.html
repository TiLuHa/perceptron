Welcome to Perceptron!

We often hear about the various problems that can be solved with neural networks: image recognition, chess computers, speech recognition, and much more.

But how does a neural network actually work? Is it really structured like the human brain? Will we no longer need programmers in the future?

We will show you the basic building block of every neural network: the perceptron - the smallest cell of a neural network.

You will see that neural networks are not as complicated as they may seem and get an idea of what tasks can be solved with a neural network and which ones cannot.

Let's get started! →

What have we learned from Jochen's pear sorting problem?

1. Simple Building Blocks

The basic building blocks of every neural network are perceptrons, which perform simple mathematical calculations. The weights and thresholds determine the results.

2. Unintelligible Inner Workings

Knowing the weights and thresholds of a neural network is of no use to a human. With this information alone, we cannot predict how a neural network will behave.

3. Self-learning Networks

If we provide a neural network with enough example data, it can learn on its own to correctly recognize and classify these datasets.

So, neural networks are not real brains that can completely replace the work of programmers. However, they can learn to make decisions like humans do, and sometimes even better.
